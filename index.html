Bridging the Bay

Transport Infrastructure, Mobility Patterns, and Equity in the Greater Bay Area

Group 19: Han SHAN, Xinyi LIN, Yezhen ZHANG

Introduction

Understanding Transport and Equity in the Greater Bay Area

The Guangdong–Hong Kong–Macao Greater Bay Area (GBA) is a megacity region characterized by administrative diversity, spatial inequality, and complex intercity dynamics. As a key national strategy, the GBA aims to enhance regional integration and global competitiveness. Transport infrastructure plays a vital role by shaping flows, reducing institutional barriers, and promoting connectivity.

This project builds an interactive mapping platform to visualize transport networks, population mobility, and accessibility equity in the GBA. By leveraging open data and web technologies, it enables dynamic spatial analysis and enhances public understanding of urban mobility challenges.

Part 1: Transport Infrastructure

Visualizing the Structure and Evolution of GBA Transport Infrastructure

This section visualizes the spatial layout and growth trends of transport infrastructure across the Greater Bay Area, revealing core–periphery imbalances and diverging patterns in passenger and freight flows.

Part 2: Mobility Patterns and Structure

Mapping Intercity Flows and Regional Connectivity

This section explores intercity mobility in the GBA using Baidu LBS data, combining flow maps and centrality analysis to reveal core–periphery patterns, temporal shifts in movement, and functional urban hierarchies.

Part 3: Accessibility and Equity

Assessing Accessibility and Spatial Justice in the GBA

This section examines who benefits from railway accessibility across the Greater Bay Area. By combining isochrones, population, and GDP data, it reveals stark spatial disparities and highlights areas of transport disadvantage.

Conclusion

This platform addresses key questions on transport accessibility, mobility patterns, and spatial equity within the Greater Bay Area. By integrating multi-source open data and interactive mapping, it reveals how infrastructure distribution, movement intensity, and socio-economic conditions intersect.

The three visual modules complement each other—mapping physical networks, dynamic flows, and equity gaps—offering a comprehensive spatial understanding of regional transport. These insights hold implications for inclusive urban planning, particularly in prioritizing underserved districts.

However, the project faces limitations: the absence of reliable data for Hong Kong and Macao, the static nature of yearly LBS data, and the lack of multi-modal coverage. Future work may adopt higher-frequency mobility datasets and expand to multimodal or cross-border analyses. As open data initiatives grow, so does the potential for more equitable, evidence-based transport planning.
