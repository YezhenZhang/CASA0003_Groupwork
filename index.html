Bridging the Bay

Transport Infrastructure, Mobility Patterns, and Equity in the Greater Bay Area

Group 19: Han SHEN, Xinyi LIN, Yezhen ZHANG

Introduction

Understanding Transport Infrastructure, Mobility Patterns, and Equity in the Greater Bay Area

The Guangdong–Hong Kong–Macao Greater Bay Area (GBA) is a megacity region characterized by administrative diversity, spatial inequality, and complex intercity dynamics. As a key national strategy, the GBA aims to enhance regional integration and global competitiveness. Transport infrastructure plays a vital role by shaping flows, reducing institutional barriers, and promoting connectivity.

This project builds an interactive mapping platform to visualize transport networks, population mobility, and accessibility equity in the GBA. By leveraging open data and web technologies, it enables dynamic spatial analysis and enhances public understanding of urban mobility challenges.

Conclusion

This project presents an interactive web-based platform for visualizing transport infrastructure, intercity mobility, and accessibility equity in the Greater Bay Area (GBA).

It yields six key findings:
(1) A core–periphery divide in infrastructure, with dense multimodal hubs in central cities but limited provision in peripheral areas;
(2) Diverging trends in modal throughput—rising rail usage, declining road traffic, and varied freight modes;
(3) Directional and temporal shifts in intercity flows driven by commuting, leisure, and holidays;
(4) Guangzhou’s dominant centrality, yet high functional connectivity in select peripheral cities;
(5) Stark accessibility disparities, especially in inland and western districts;
(6) A spatial overlap between low accessibility and low income, forming “double disadvantage” zones.

However, the project has three main limitations:
(1) incomplete datasets—missing annual throughput data for some years, and lacking LBS mobility data and district-level GDP per capita for Hong Kong and Macao;
(2) Simplified accessibility measures—excluding metro connections, service frequency, and fare affordability;
(3) Minor interface issues—map zooming may disrupt user experience during scrolling.

These limitations highlight the need for improved cross-boundary data sharing, comprehensive multimodal accessibility metrics, and enhanced UI control.
